## Why These Scenarios?

### Calibrated to 34 Years of USS History

Every scenario in this framework is anchored to actual USS performance from 1990-2023. We didn't arbitrarily pick "conservative" and "optimistic" labels. Instead, we mapped scenarios to specific historical percentiles based on what actually happened.

**Historical Percentile Mapping:**

| Scenario | Historical Percentile | Historical Frequency |
|----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Severe Downturn | 0-25th percentile | 24% of years (8-9 years) |
| Downside | 25-40th percentile | 30% of years |
| Base Case | 50th percentile (median) | 30% of years |
| Above Average | 75-90th percentile | 10% of years |
| Optimistic | 90th+ percentile | 5% of years |

### Probability Weights Reflect Actual Frequency

The probability weights assigned to each scenario aren't assumptions - they're derived from counting how often USS experienced each type of year:

- **8-9 severe years** (2009, 2015, 2020, 1991, 2001, 2002, 2003, 2016) = 24% frequency
- **~10 below-average years** = 30% frequency
- **~10 mid-cycle years** = 30% frequency
- **~4 strong years** (2017, 2018, etc.) = 10% frequency
- **~2 peak years** (2021, 2022) = 5% frequency

### Why Single "Base Case" Analysis Falls Short

Traditional DCF analysis picks one scenario and calls it "base case." For USS, this approach is dangerous:

1. **Extreme cyclicality**: EBITDA ranged from -$1,032M to +$5,396M (coefficient of variation: 120%)
2. **Frequent losses**: 15 out of 34 years were unprofitable (44%)
3. **Non-normal distribution**: Steel company outcomes cluster at extremes, not the middle

A single-scenario DCF is like forecasting weather by assuming it will be partly cloudy every day - you'll be wrong most of the time.

## The Five Probability-Weighted Scenarios

### 1. Severe Downturn (25% Probability)

**The Story**

A severe recession hits the U.S. economy. Automotive production collapses 20-30% as consumers delay vehicle purchases. Construction spending plummets 15-25%. Oil & gas companies slash capital expenditures, devastating the tubular segment. Import competition intensifies as domestic prices fall and global steel flows to the U.S. market. USS suspends all discretionary capital projects and implements crisis cost reductions.

**Historical Precedent**

| Crisis Year | What Happened | Impact on USS |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| **2008-09** | Financial crisis, automotive bailout | Revenue -53%, EBITDA -$1,406M |
| **2015-16** | Import surge, Chinese overcapacity | Revenue -34%, EBITDA $19M (0.2% margin) |
| **2020** | COVID demand shock | Revenue -25%, EBITDA -$69M |

**Key Assumptions**

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |

| Volume Factor | 75-85% | Demand collapse across segments |

**Valuation Outcome**

| Metric | Value |

*Note: USS Standalone value of $0.00 reflects that enterprise value falls below debt obligations in this scenario. Shareholders have limited liability - they cannot lose more than their investment.*

**Why 25% Probability?**

This scenario occurred in 8-9 years out of 34 (24% frequency). These aren't theoretical stress tests - they're actual historical outcomes. Given USS's leverage to economic cycles and energy markets, severe downturns happen roughly once every four years on average.

## Scenario Selection Guide

### Decision Tree: When to Use Which Scenario

```
What question are you trying to answer?
    |
    +-- "What is USS's fair value?" --> Use Probability-Weighted Expected Value
    |
    +-- "What if the economy enters recession?" --> Use Severe Downturn
    |
    +-- "What do Wall Street analysts think?" --> Use Wall Street Consensus
    |
    +-- "What's the upside case?" --> Use Optimistic or Above Average
    |
    +-- "What does the merger create for Nippon?" --> Use NSA Mandated CapEx
    |
    +-- "What's the most likely single outcome?" --> Use Base Case (but recognize 30% probability)
```

### Recommended Approach by Stakeholder

| Stakeholder | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------|
| **USS Board** | Probability-weighted | Must protect all shareholders; downside risk is critical |
| **Nippon Steel** | NSA Mandated CapEx | Evaluating strategic value from investment program |
| **Shareholders** | Probability-weighted | Risk-adjusted fair value determines deal attractiveness |
| **Regulators** | Full range + Severe Downturn | Must understand both upside and crisis scenarios |
| **Analysts** | All scenarios | Compare to their own models, validate assumptions |

### The Bottom Line

**For fair value assessment, always use the probability-weighted approach.** Single-scenario analysis systematically underestimates downside risk in cyclical industries. The 86% premium of Nippon's $55 offer over risk-adjusted value only becomes apparent when you account for the 25% probability of severe downturn.

### S.4 IRP-Adjusted WACC Calculation

Nippon's cost of acquiring USS assets in USD is calculated using Interest Rate Parity:

**Formula:**
```
Nippon USD WACC = Nippon JPY WACC + (US 10Y - Japan 10Y)
```

**Standard Parameters:**
- US 10-Year Treasury: 4.25%
- Japan 10-Year JGB: 0.75%
- IRP Adjustment: +3.50%
- Nippon Equity Risk Premium: 4.75%
- Nippon Credit Spread: 0.75%
- Nippon Debt Ratio: 35%
- Nippon Tax Rate: 30%

**Result:** IRP-Adjusted Nippon WACC in USD = ~7.95% (vs. USS 10.9%)

This WACC advantage explains why Nippon can pay $55/share while creating value - their cost of capital is structurally lower.

**USS WACC Formula:**
```
Cost of Equity = Risk-Free + (Beta × ERP) + Size Premium
               = 3.88% + (1.45 × 5.50%) + 1.00% = 12.86%

Cost of Debt (after-tax) = 7.18% × (1 - 16.9%) = 5.97%

WACC = (E/V × Re) + (D/V × Rd)
     = (71.1% × 12.86%) + (28.9% × 5.97%) = 10.70%
```

**Nippon Steel WACC Calculation (7.95% USD-adjusted)**

| Input | Value | Source |
|-------|-------|--------|
| Japan Risk-Free Rate (10Y JGB) | 0.61% | Bank of Japan / Ministry of Finance Japan |

| Debt Ratio | 35% | Tokyo Stock Exchange, Nippon Steel FY2023 Annual Report |
| Japan Corporate Tax Rate | 30% | Japan Ministry of Finance |

**Nippon WACC Formula:**
```
JPY Cost of Equity = JGB + (Beta × Japan ERP)
                   = 0.61% + (1.15 × 5.50%) = 6.94%

JPY Cost of Debt (after-tax) = 1.41% × (1 - 30%) = 0.99%

JPY WACC = (65% × 6.94%) + (35% × 0.99%) = 4.55%

USD WACC = JPY WACC + (US 10Y - Japan 10Y)
         = 4.55% + (3.88% - 0.61%) = 7.82%
```

*Note: The IRP adjustment converts Nippon's yen-denominated cost of capital to USD terms for valuing USD-denominated USS cash flows.*
