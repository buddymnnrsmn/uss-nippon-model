# LBO Stress Testing: Downside Scenarios for USS Acquisition

## Executive Summary

This document analyzes historical steel industry downturns to develop stress scenarios for LBO debt service capacity analysis. Key findings:

- **2008-2009 Financial Crisis**: Steel prices fell 62% from peak to trough ($1,000 to $380/ton HRC), production dropped 37%, and EBITDA margins compressed to near-zero or negative
- **2015-2016 Steel Crisis**: Industry EBITDA margins fell to 8% (from 16% mid-cycle), with 700M tonnes of global overcapacity; USS EBITDA declined to $1.25B (from $2.1B baseline)
- **Minimum Debt Service Capacity**: At 3.5x leverage on a $14.8B acquisition ($10.6B debt), USS needs minimum EBITDA of $1.2B annually to maintain 1.5x interest coverage
- **Stress Test Recommendation**: Model downside scenarios with HRC at $500/ton (2009 trough levels), 30% volume contraction, and EBITDA margins of 5-8%

## 1. Historical Steel Cycle Analysis

### 1.1 The 2008-2009 Financial Crisis

#### Market Conditions

The 2008-2009 financial crisis represented the most severe steel industry downturn in modern history, driven by the collapse of construction and automotive demand.

**Price Collapse:**
- Summer 2008: HRC prices exceeded **$1,000/ton** with mills operating at 90% capacity
- Early 2009: HRC prices bottomed at **$380/ton**
- **Total decline: 62%** from peak to trough in 19 weeks

**Production Decline:**
- U.S. steel production fell **37.2%** between January and November 2008
- Capacity utilization plunged from **90% to 33%** in 19 weeks
- The bottom fell out between August-December 2008 as the financial crisis and auto production collapse hit simultaneously

**Company-Specific Impacts:**

| Company | Stock Decline | Operational Impact | Financial Result |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|------------------|
| U.S. Steel | -69% | 675 layoffs in U.S./Canada | Record Q3 profits to losses |
| AK Steel | -80% | Plants closed in OH/KY, 1,190 layoffs | Severe losses |
| ArcelorMittal | N/A | Global capacity cuts | $1.5B operating loss (Q1 2009 vs $3.6B profit Q1 2008) |

**EBITDA Impact:**

ArcelorMittal's Net Debt/EBITDA ratio deteriorated from **1.1x** (Dec 2008) to **1.3x** (March 2009), indicating EBITDA decline even as debt remained relatively stable. The operating loss of $1.5B in Q1 2009 (vs $3.6B profit in Q1 2008) suggests a **$5.1B swing** in quarterly operating income, implying EBITDA margins went **negative** during the trough.

**Recovery Timeline:**
- Trough: Q1-Q2 2009
- Recovery began: Late 2009
- Return to normalized pricing: 2011-2012
- **Duration: ~24-30 months** from peak to recovery

#### Implications for USS LBO

In a 2008-style crisis, USS would face:
- **HRC prices: $380-500/ton** (vs $680 base case)
- **Volume decline: 30-40%** across all segments
- **EBITDA margins: 0-5%** or negative
- **Working capital release**: $500M-$1B as inventory liquidates
- **CapEx cuts**: All discretionary projects halted

### 1.2 The 2015-2016 Steel Industry Crisis

#### Market Conditions

The 2015-2016 crisis was driven by Chinese overcapacity and weak global demand, representing a less severe but prolonged downturn compared to 2008-2009.

**Price Decline:**
- Steel prices fell below **$300/tonne** globally in early 2016
- U.S. HRC prices declined approximately **20-25%** from 2014 levels
- Less severe than 2008-2009 but more persistent

**Production Decline:**
- U.S. domestic shipments: **87M tons** in 2015, down **12%** from 2014
- Industry described as worse than late 1990s crisis in some financial metrics

**Global Overcapacity:**
- OECD estimated **700M tonnes** of excess global steel capacity
- China accounted for almost half of world steelmaking and over half of overcapacity
- This structural overcapacity kept prices depressed for an extended period

**EBITDA Margins:**
- Industry EBITDA margins declined to **8%** in 2015 (from 16% in 2002-2008 boom)
- Margins improved slightly to **11%** in 2016 but remained well below mid-cycle
- This represented a **50% compression** from peak margins

**USS-Specific Financial Impact:**

| Metric | 2015 | 2016 | Analysis |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| EBITDA | $1,400M | $1,250M | 11% decline YoY |
| Total Debt | $3,138M | $3,031M | Slight deleveraging |
| Net Debt | $2,383M | $1,516M | Cash conservation |
| Net Debt/EBITDA | 1.7x | 1.2x | Improved through cash build |

USS established a **full valuation allowance** in Q4 2015, indicating significant financial distress and expectation of near-term losses. The company avoided bankruptcy but faced severe margin compression.

**Recovery Timeline:**
- Trough: 2015-2016
- Recovery began: 2017 (Section 232 tariffs announced)
- Full recovery: 2021-2023 (post-COVID surge)
- **Duration: ~24-36 months** of depressed conditions

#### Implications for USS LBO

In a 2015-2016 style crisis, USS would face:
- **HRC prices: $500-600/ton** (moderately depressed)
- **Volume decline: 10-15%** (less severe than 2008)
- **EBITDA margins: 8-10%** (compressed but positive)
- **EBITDA: $1.2-1.5B** (vs $2.1B base case)
- **Working capital: Neutral** (slower but not collapse)
- **CapEx: Reduced** but maintenance continues

## 3. Working Capital Swings in Downturns

### 3.1 Working Capital Dynamics

Working capital behavior varies significantly between rapid crashes (2008-2009) and gradual downturns (2015-2016).

**Formula:**
Net Working Capital = (DSO + DIH - DPO) × Daily Revenue

Where:
- **DSO**: Days Sales Outstanding (receivables)
- **DIH**: Days Inventory Held
- **DPO**: Days Payable Outstanding

**Base Case USS Working Capital (Mid-Cycle):**

| Segment | DSO | DIH | DPO | Cash Conversion Cycle | Daily Revenue (2023) | NWC |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|----------------------|---------------------|-----|
| Flat-Rolled | 28 | 55 | 60 | 23 days | $24.6M | $565M |
| Mini Mill | 22 | 40 | 70 | -8 days | $5.8M | -$46M |
| USSE | 30 | 50 | 65 | 15 days | $9.3M | $140M |
| Tubular | 35 | 60 | 55 | 40 days | $4.1M | $164M |
| **Total** | **~27** | **~52** | **~62** | **~17 days** | **$43.8M** | **$823M** |

**Source:** [Understanding the Working Capital Cycle](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/working-capital-cycle/)

### 3.2 Crisis Scenario: Rapid Downturn (2008-2009 Style)

In a rapid collapse, working capital becomes a **cash source** as:
- Inventory liquidates rapidly (DIH falls 30-40%)
- Receivables collect faster as revenue plummets (DSO extends but absolute $ falls)
- Payables stretch (DPO extends as suppliers get nervous)

**Stressed Working Capital:**

| Segment | DSO | DIH | DPO | Cash Cycle | Daily Revenue (Crisis) | NWC | Change |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|-----------|----------------------|-----|--------|
| Flat-Rolled | 35 | 35 | 75 | -5 days | $6.4M | -$32M | **+$597M** |
| Mini Mill | 28 | 25 | 80 | -27 days | $1.5M | -$41M | **+$5M** |
| USSE | 40 | 35 | 80 | -5 days | $2.4M | -$12M | **+$152M** |
| Tubular | 45 | 40 | 65 | 20 days | $1.0M | $20M | **+$144M** |
| **Total** | **~37** | **~34** | **~75** | **~-4 days** | **$11.4M** | **-$65M** | **+$898M** |

**Working Capital Release: ~$900M** in a severe crisis

This cash release partially offsets EBITDA collapse but is a one-time benefit. As business stabilizes, working capital must rebuild, creating a cash drag during recovery.

### 3.3 Gradual Downturn (2015-2016 Style)

In a gradual downturn, working capital impact is **muted**:
- Inventory adjusts slowly (DIH falls 10-15%)
- Receivables compress with revenue but DSO stays stable
- Payables adjust proportionally

**Moderate Stress Working Capital:**

| Segment | DSO | DIH | DPO | Cash Cycle | Daily Revenue | NWC | Change |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|-----------|--------------|-----|--------|
| Flat-Rolled | 30 | 45 | 65 | 10 days | $12.2M | $122M | **+$443M** |
| Mini Mill | 24 | 35 | 72 | -13 days | $2.9M | -$38M | **+$8M** |
| USSE | 32 | 42 | 68 | 6 days | $4.6M | $28M | **+$112M** |
| Tubular | 38 | 50 | 58 | 30 days | $2.0M | $60M | **+$104M** |
| **Total** | **~31** | **~43** | **~66** | **~8 days** | **$21.7M** | **$172M** | **+$651M** |

**Working Capital Release: ~$650M** in moderate stress

**Sources:**
- [Working Capital Cycle | Formula + Calculator](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/working-capital-cycle/)
- [Cash Conversion Cycle | Working Capital Management](https://treasuryxl.com/what-is-working-capital-management/cash-conversion-cycle/)

### 3.4 LBO Implications

For LBO stress testing:
1. **Cash Cushion**: Working capital release provides 6-12 months of debt service in severe crisis
2. **One-Time Benefit**: Cannot rely on WC release beyond initial adjustment
3. **Recovery Drag**: Working capital rebuild during recovery reduces FCF available for debt paydown
4. **Covenant Flexibility**: WC-based covenants may provide relief (lower total assets = lower NWC requirements)

## 4. Minimum Coverage Ratios and Debt Service Capacity

### 4.1 LBO Debt Structure Assumptions

**Acquisition Details:**
- Enterprise Value: $14.8B (USS equity at $55/share = $12.4B + $2.4B net debt)
- Equity Contribution: 30% = $4.4B
- Debt Financing: 70% = $10.3B

**Debt Structure (Typical LBO):**

| Tranche | Amount | % of Total Debt | Interest Rate | Maturity | Annual Interest |
|---------|--------|----------------|---------------|----------|----------------|
| Revolver | $1.0B | 10% | SOFR + 3.50% = 7.50% | 5 years | $75M |
| Term Loan B | $5.2B | 50% | SOFR + 4.25% = 8.25% | 7 years | $429M |
| Second Lien | $2.1B | 20% | SOFR + 6.50% = 10.50% | 8 years | $221M |
| High Yield Bonds | $2.1B | 20% | 9.50% | 10 years | $200M |
| **Total Debt** | **$10.4B** | **100%** | **Blended 9.0%** | | **$925M** |

**Amortization:**
- Term Loan B: 1% annual mandatory amortization = $52M/year
- Other tranches: Bullet maturity
- **Total Annual Debt Service**: Interest $925M + Amortization $52M = **$977M**

### 4.2 Lender Covenant Requirements

Typical LBO debt covenants for steel industry:

| Covenant | Investment Grade | Leveraged Loan | High Yield | Critical Threshold |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Total Debt/EBITDA** | ≤ 3.0x | ≤ 5.0x | ≤ 6.0x | 6.5x+ triggers default |
| **Senior Debt/EBITDA** | ≤ 2.0x | ≤ 3.5x | ≤ 4.0x | 4.5x+ triggers springing lien |
| **Interest Coverage** | ≥ 3.5x | ≥ 2.0x | ≥ 1.75x | < 1.5x bankruptcy risk |
| **Fixed Charge Coverage** | ≥ 2.5x | ≥ 1.25x | ≥ 1.1x | < 1.0x default |

**Sources:**
- [Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) - Financial Edge](https://www.fe.training/free-resources/project-finance/debt-service-coverage-ratio-dscr/)
- [Average net debt to EBITDA ratio by industry](https://fullratio.com/net-debt-to-ebitda-by-industry)

### 4.3 Minimum EBITDA Requirements

#### Scenario 1: 3.5x Leverage Covenant

If debt covenant requires **Total Debt/EBITDA ≤ 3.5x**:

**Minimum EBITDA** = Total Debt ÷ 3.5 = $10.4B ÷ 3.5 = **$2,971M**

This is **above** USS's 2023 actual EBITDA of $2,139M, suggesting 3.5x leverage would be **too aggressive** for USS LBO.

#### Scenario 2: 5.0x Leverage Covenant (More Realistic)

If debt covenant requires **Total Debt/EBITDA ≤ 5.0x**:

**Minimum EBITDA** = $10.4B ÷ 5.0 = **$2,080M**

This is close to USS's 2023 EBITDA, providing minimal cushion.

#### Scenario 3: Interest Coverage Requirement

If debt covenant requires **EBITDA/Interest Expense ≥ 2.0x**:

**Minimum EBITDA** = Interest Expense × 2.0 = $925M × 2.0 = **$1,850M**

This provides some downside cushion (14% below 2023 EBITDA).

#### Scenario 4: Fixed Charge Coverage

If debt covenant requires **EBITDA/(Interest + CapEx + Dividends) ≥ 1.25x**:

Assume:
- Interest: $925M
- Maintenance CapEx: $600M (3.8% of revenue)
- Dividends: $0 (suspended in LBO)
- Fixed Charges: $1,525M

**Minimum EBITDA** = $1,525M × 1.25 = **$1,906M**

### 4.4 Stress Testing Results

| Scenario | EBITDA | Total Leverage | Interest Coverage | Fixed Charge Coverage | Covenant Status |
|----------|--------|---------------|-------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Base Case (2023)** | $2,139M | 4.9x | 2.31x | 1.40x | **PASS** |
| **Mild Stress** | $990M | 10.5x | 1.07x | 0.65x | **FAIL** |
| **Moderate Stress (2015-16)** | $730M | 14.2x | 0.79x | 0.48x | **DEFAULT** |
| **Severe Stress (2008-09)** | $150M | 69.3x | 0.16x | 0.10x | **BANKRUPTCY** |

**Key Insight:** USS LBO can only survive a **mild stress** scenario (EBITDA > $1,850M, 14% decline from base). A 2015-2016 style downturn would trigger covenant defaults. A 2008-2009 crisis would lead to bankruptcy without emergency restructuring.

### 4.5 Downside Protection Strategies

To improve downside resilience:

1. **Lower Leverage**: Target 4.0x Total Debt/EBITDA at entry
   - Reduces debt to $8.6B
   - Increases equity to $6.2B (42% equity check)
   - Minimum EBITDA for 5.0x covenant: $1,720M (provides 20% cushion)

2. **Asset-Backed Revolver**: $1-2B ABL against inventory/receivables
   - Provides liquidity cushion when EBITDA drops
   - Less restrictive covenants (based on borrowing base, not leverage)

3. **PIK Toggle Notes**: $1-2B of high-yield with payment-in-kind option
   - Can defer cash interest in downturn
   - Preserves liquidity for operations

4. **Equity Cushion**: 40% equity (vs 30%)
   - Reduces interest burden by $100M+/year
   - Improves return hurdles but critical for safety

5. **Covenant-Lite Structure**: Remove or relax leverage/coverage covenants
   - Increases interest cost by 50-100bps
   - Provides flexibility in downturn but reduces lender protections

### 5.4 Key Findings Summary

1. **LBO Feasibility:** USS LBO at 4.9x leverage is **highly vulnerable** to steel price downturns. Historical crises suggest 40-60% probability of covenant breach within first 5 years.

2. **Recommended Entry Leverage:** **4.0x Total Debt/EBITDA** ($8.6B debt, $6.2B equity) provides minimum downside protection. This requires 42% equity contribution.

3. **Minimum Operating EBITDA:** USS needs **$1,200M EBITDA** minimum to service debt at 3.5x leverage with 1.5x interest coverage. This is 44% below 2023 actuals but 60% above 2015-2016 trough.

4. **Crisis Survival:** In a 2008-2009 style crisis, even conservative 4.0x entry leverage would fail. USS LBO requires:
   - $2B+ liquidity cushion (revolver + cash)
   - Covenant-lite or highly flexible structure
   - Sponsor commitment for equity injections
   - Asset sale optionality ($2-3B of non-core assets)

5. **Strategic Alternative:** The Nippon acquisition at $55/share ($12.4B equity value) with **zero acquisition debt** is structurally superior to any LBO structure. Nippon avoids:
   - $900M+ annual interest expense
   - Covenant restrictions limiting investment
   - Forced asset sales in downturns
   - Bankruptcy risk in crisis scenarios
