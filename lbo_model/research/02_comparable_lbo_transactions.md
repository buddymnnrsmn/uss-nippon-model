# Comparable LBO Transactions: Steel and Heavy Industrial Sectors

## Executive Summary

This research analyzes leveraged buyout transactions in steel manufacturing, heavy industrials, and capital-intensive manufacturing sectors from 2015-2025. While specific steel manufacturing LBOs are limited due to the sector's capital-intensive nature and cyclicality, this report identifies relevant industrial manufacturing transactions and provides market-based benchmarks for entry multiples, leverage ratios, and debt structures.

**Key Findings:**
- Entry multiples for capital-intensive manufacturing: **6.0x - 12.0x EV/EBITDA** (industrials typically at mid-to-high single digits)
- Typical leverage ratios (2015-2019): **4.0x - 6.0x Total Debt/EBITDA**
- Post-2022 leverage ratios: **4.0x - 6.0x Total Debt/EBITDA** (with senior debt at 3.0x-4.5x)
- Equity contribution has increased from ~30% to over 50% in recent years due to higher interest rates

## 2. Analysis of Entry Multiples

### Median and Range by Period

**2013-2019 Period:**
- **Entry Multiples:** 6.0x - 12.0x EV/EBITDA
- **Industrial Manufacturing Range:** 7.0x - 9.0x EV/EBITDA
- **Peak (2021):** Purchase price multiples reached record highs before correction
- **2024:** Median EV/EBITDA for buyouts >$1B at 15.5x (vs. 12.8x for <$1B deals, <10x for <$100M deals)

**Capital-Intensive Manufacturing Considerations:**
- Traditional manufacturing multiples sit lower due to product line risk and higher capex requirements
- Asset-light businesses (e.g., software) command significantly higher multiples
- EV/EBIT (vs. EV/EBITDA) is preferred in capital-intensive industries as it accounts for depreciation

**Sector-Specific Observations:**
- Steel sector median EV/EBITDA (2024): ~10.6x for metals & electronics sector globally
- U.S. Steel EV/EBITDA (Jan 2025): 7.63x (public market comparable)
- Industrial equipment deals: 7.0x - 9.0x range typical

### Factors Influencing Multiples

1. **Quality of Earnings:** Stable, recurring revenue streams command higher multiples
2. **Market Position:** Leading market positions justify premium valuations
3. **Growth Prospects:** Companies with organic growth potential achieve higher entry multiples
4. **Asset Intensity:** Higher capex requirements depress multiples
5. **Cyclicality:** Steel and heavy industrials face cyclical demand, limiting multiples
6. **Customer Diversification:** Broad customer base reduces concentration risk

## 3. Leverage Ratios and Debt Structure

### Typical Leverage Levels

**Pre-2022 Environment (Low Interest Rates):**
- **Total Debt/EBITDA:** 5.0x - 6.0x (occasionally up to 7.0x for aggressive deals)
- **Senior Debt:** 3.5x - 4.5x EBITDA
- **Subordinated/Mezzanine Debt:** 1.5x - 2.5x EBITDA
- **Equity Contribution:** 20-30% of purchase price

**Post-2022 Environment (High Interest Rates):**
- **Total Debt/EBITDA:** 4.0x - 6.0x
- **Senior Debt:** 3.0x - 4.5x EBITDA
- **Subordinated/Mezzanine Debt:** 1.0x - 1.5x EBITDA
- **Equity Contribution:** 50%+ of purchase price (first time >50% since 1997)
- **Average LBO Interest Rates:** Doubled from 4-5% (2020) to 9-11% (2023-2024)

### Debt Structure Components

**Senior Debt (Secured):**
- First lien on company assets
- Typically term loan B structure
- Lower interest rates (SOFR + 300-500 bps historically)
- Covenants: maintenance or incurrence-based
- Current environment: SOFR + 450-600 bps

**High Yield Bonds:**
- Unsecured debt with higher interest rates
- 8-10 year maturity typical
- Used for larger transactions
- PIK (payment-in-kind) toggle features in some structures

**Mezzanine Debt:**
- Hybrid debt-equity instrument
- Junior to senior and high-yield debt
- Interest rates: 12-18% typical
- Often includes equity warrants/kickers
- Provided by specialized mezzanine funds or hedge funds

**Equity:**
- Common equity from sponsor
- Management rollover equity
- Co-investment from management team
- Recent deals: 50%+ equity contribution

### Real Transaction Example

**Gardner Denver (KKR, 2013):**
- Total Enterprise Value: $3.9B
- Assumed debt: ~$400M existing
- Estimated new debt: ~$2.7B (implying ~6.0x leverage on ~$450M EBITDA)
- Structure: Term Loan B + Senior Notes
- Exit: IPO in 2017, later merged with Ingersoll Rand Industrial (2020)

## 4. Success Factors and Lessons Learned

### Value Creation Drivers

Private equity firms typically generate returns through three levers:

1. **Operational Improvements (EBITDA Growth)**
   - Manufacturing efficiency initiatives
   - Procurement optimization
   - Footprint rationalization
   - Product mix enhancement
   - Working capital optimization

2. **Debt Paydown (Deleveraging)**
   - Using free cash flow to reduce debt
   - De-levering from 6.0x to 3.0x over 5-7 years typical
   - Refinancing at favorable terms

3. **Multiple Expansion**
   - Strategic repositioning (e.g., manufacturing to tech-enabled services)
   - Market share gains
   - Geographic expansion
   - Add-on acquisitions ("buy-and-build")
   - Improved investor perception

### Key Success Factors in Industrial LBOs

1. **Strong Management Teams**
   - Experienced operators with sector expertise
   - Proven ability to execute operational improvements
   - Alignment through equity participation

2. **Defensible Market Positions**
   - Leading positions in niche markets
   - High switching costs for customers
   - Technical expertise or IP advantages

3. **Stable Cash Flow Generation**
   - Essential for debt service in leveraged structures
   - Diversified customer base reduces concentration risk
   - Long-term contracts or recurring revenue preferred

4. **Operational Improvement Opportunities**
   - Manufacturing footprint optimization
   - Procurement savings through scale
   - Portfolio rationalization
   - Pricing power improvements

5. **Add-On Acquisition Potential**
   - Fragmented industries provide bolt-on opportunities
   - Multiple arbitrage: acquire smaller companies at lower multiples
   - Geographic or product line expansion

### Challenges in Capital-Intensive Manufacturing LBOs

1. **Cyclicality**
   - Economic downturns impact demand significantly
   - Steel sector particularly exposed to construction and automotive cycles
   - Requires conservative leverage to weather downturns

2. **High Capex Requirements**
   - Maintenance capex reduces free cash flow available for debt paydown
   - Growth capex competes with deleveraging priorities
   - Older assets may require significant modernization investment

3. **Environmental and Regulatory Risks**
   - Steel and heavy manufacturing face environmental regulations
   - Compliance costs and potential liabilities
   - Carbon emissions regulations increasing

4. **Commodity Price Exposure**
   - Raw material costs (iron ore, scrap, energy) volatile
   - Limited ability to pass through costs in competitive markets
   - Requires sophisticated hedging strategies

5. **Global Competition**
   - Low-cost producers in Asia
   - Trade policy and tariff uncertainties
   - Currency fluctuation impacts

### Lessons from Failed or Challenged Deals

While specific failed steel LBO data is limited, general lessons include:

1. **Over-Leverage Risk:** Deals with >6.5x leverage struggled during 2008-2009 and 2020 downturns
2. **Insufficient Due Diligence:** Underestimating capex requirements or environmental liabilities
3. **Market Timing:** Entering at peak multiples with assumption of continued expansion
4. **Integration Challenges:** Roll-up strategies failed when integration costs exceeded synergy benefits
5. **Technology Disruption:** Failure to invest in modernization and automation

## 5. Market Context and Trends (2015-2025)

### Deal Volume and Activity

**2015-2019: Peak Private Equity Era**
- Low interest rates facilitated aggressive leverage
- High deal volumes and competition for assets
- Average deal size reached $1B in 2021
- Technology and healthcare dominated deal count, but industrials maintained steady activity

**2020-2021: Pandemic Impact and Recovery**
- Initial freeze in Q1-Q2 2020
- Rapid recovery driven by fiscal stimulus
- Industrial sector seen as "flight to safety" with stable cash flows
- Supply chain disruptions created opportunities and challenges

**2022-2024: Rising Rate Environment**
- Interest rates doubled, making leverage more expensive
- Equity contributions increased from 30% to >50%
- Deal volumes decreased by ~30-40% from peak
- Focus shifted to operational improvements vs. financial engineering
- Megadeals (>$5B) cut nearly in half

**2025 Outlook:**
- Continued selectivity in deal-making
- Focus on sectors with pricing power to offset interest costs
- Industrials sector maintains appeal for defensive characteristics
- Potential increase in secondary buyouts and carve-outs

### Why Steel LBOs Are Rare

1. **High Capital Intensity:** Steel mills require $500M+ in capex for modernization
2. **Cyclicality:** Difficult to forecast cash flows for debt service
3. **Commodity Nature:** Limited differentiation and pricing power
4. **Global Overcapacity:** Structural challenges in steel markets
5. **Environmental Liabilities:** Significant regulatory and remediation risks
6. **Size:** Major steel companies too large for all but mega-buyout funds

**Alternative Approaches:**
- Private equity focuses on downstream steel processors and fabricators (lower capex, higher value-add)
- Specialty steel producers with niche applications
- Service centers and distributors (asset-light, steady margins)
- Steel-adjacent industries (building products, engineered components)

## 6. Implications for USS LBO Analysis

Based on the comparable transaction research, the following benchmarks are recommended for the USS LBO model:

### Entry Multiple Range
- **Base Case:** 7.0x - 8.0x EV/EBITDA
- **Rationale:** USS is a capital-intensive, cyclical steel manufacturer. Comparable industrials trade at 7-9x. Current steel sector median at 10.6x, but LBO multiples typically below public market.

### Leverage Structure
- **Total Debt/EBITDA:** 4.5x - 5.5x
- **Senior Debt:** 3.0x - 3.5x EBITDA
- **Subordinated Debt:** 1.5x - 2.0x EBITDA
- **Equity:** 45-50% of purchase price
- **Rationale:** Conservative leverage given cyclicality and current high interest rate environment

### Debt Pricing Assumptions
- **Senior Debt:** SOFR + 450-550 bps (8.5-9.5% all-in)
- **Subordinated Debt:** 12-14%
- **Rationale:** Reflects current market conditions for industrial LBOs

### Exit Multiple
- **Base Case:** 7.5x - 8.5x EV/EBITDA
- **Upside Case:** 9.0x - 10.0x (if operational improvements successfully implemented)
- **Downside Case:** 6.0x - 7.0x (if industry conditions deteriorate)

### Expected Returns
- **Target IRR:** 18-22% (typical PE target for mid-market industrials)
- **Cash-on-Cash Multiple:** 2.5x - 3.0x over 5-7 year hold period

## 8. Research Limitations and Data Gaps

### Key Limitations

1. **Limited Steel-Specific LBO Data:**
   - Large integrated steel producers (ArcelorMittal, Tata Steel, Nucor) remained publicly traded throughout 2015-2025
   - No major private equity buyouts of pure-play steel manufacturers identified
   - Research focused on adjacent heavy industrial and manufacturing sectors

2. **Proprietary Transaction Details:**
   - Specific leverage ratios and debt structures often not publicly disclosed
   - Exit multiples and realized returns typically confidential
   - Detailed operational improvement initiatives not public

3. **Market Timing:**
   - Most well-documented deals occurred in 2013-2019 period
   - Limited data on 2022-2024 industrial LBOs due to market slowdown
   - Current deals still in hold period, limiting exit performance data

4. **Geographic Focus:**
   - Research primarily covers North American and European transactions
   - Limited visibility into Asian industrial LBOs
   - USS's unique position in U.S. steel market may not have direct comparables

### Recommended Additional Research

1. **Downstream Steel Processing:**
   - Service centers, steel fabricators, and processors
   - May provide more relevant comparable transactions
   - Lower capital intensity and more stable margins

2. **Carve-Out Transactions:**
   - Steel divisions sold by diversified industrials
   - Corporate divestitures with LBO-type financing
   - May reveal valuation benchmarks

3. **International Precedents:**
   - European steel restructurings
   - Asian steel sector consolidation
   - Government-backed transactions

4. **Credit Market Analysis:**
   - Current bank appetite for steel sector lending
   - High-yield market conditions for industrial issuers
   - Private credit fund activity in capital-intensive sectors

## Appendix A: Glossary of Terms

**EV/EBITDA:** Enterprise Value divided by Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. Primary valuation multiple used in LBOs.

**Total Debt/EBITDA:** Leverage ratio measuring total debt burden relative to operating cash flow (approximation). Also called "Debt Multiple."

**Senior Debt:** First-lien secured debt with priority claim on assets in bankruptcy. Lowest cost of debt capital.

**Mezzanine Debt:** Subordinated debt with equity-like features (warrants, PIK interest). Higher cost than senior debt, lower than equity.

**PIK (Payment-in-Kind):** Interest that accrues and compounds rather than being paid in cash. Used to preserve cash flow for debt paydown.

**Multiple Expansion:** Increase in valuation multiple from entry to exit. E.g., buying at 7x and selling at 9x EBITDA.

**Deleveraging:** Reduction in debt balance relative to EBITDA through debt paydown or EBITDA growth.

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return):** Annualized return accounting for timing of cash flows. PE industry standard performance metric.

**MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital):** Total cash returned divided by total cash invested. Complements IRR by showing absolute return.

**Add-On Acquisition:** Smaller acquisition made by portfolio company to consolidate market or expand capabilities. Also called "bolt-on."

**Covenant:** Loan terms restricting borrower actions or requiring minimum financial performance. Protects lenders.

**SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate):** Replaced LIBOR as benchmark interest rate. Base rate for most leveraged loans.
